A typical action potential begins with an electrical stimulus that opens voltage-gated sodium channels on the neuron's membrane. This allows positively charged ions to flow into the neuron, which causes the cell to become more positive inside. This change in polarity creates an electrical current that travels down the neuron's axon to its terminals. When an animal threatens another, it usually does one of two things: stands up tall and makes itself look big, or puts out its teeth and claws. These signals are meant to scare the other animal away, and they work because the other animal knows that if it doesn't back down, it might get hurt.In the same way, when cells send biological signals to each other, they're using their "teeth" and "claws" to try to scare the other cell away. The difference is that in this case, the cells aren't actually trying to hurt each other – they're just trying to get each others' attention.
